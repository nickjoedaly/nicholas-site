Frequently asked questions
What does the commission process typically look like?+
After receiving your inquiry, I'll schedule a conversation (video call or phone) to discuss your vision, the ensemble, timeline, and any creative constraints. If we decide to move forward, I draft a contract outlining the scope, fee, payment schedule, and delivery milestones. During composition, I share drafts at agreed-upon checkpoints for feedback. The final deliverable includes a full score, individual parts, and any supplementary materials (electronics patches, performance notes).
How far in advance should I reach out?+
Ideally 6–12 months before the desired premiere date, though shorter timelines are sometimes possible depending on the scope of the work and my current schedule. For orchestral works or large-scale projects, 12–18 months is more realistic. I'm happy to discuss your timeline even if it feels tight — sometimes we can find a way to make it work.
Do you write for electronics or non-traditional forces?+
Absolutely. A significant portion of my catalog involves live electronics, and I've written for a wide range of non-Western and non-standard instruments. If your project involves unusual instrumentation, extended techniques, or technology, that's often where the most interesting creative work happens. I provide all necessary electronics patches, setup documentation, and technical support.
What rights do I receive as a commissioner?+
Standard commissioning agreements typically include exclusive performance rights for a defined period (usually 6–12 months from the premiere), after which the work enters my general catalog for others to perform under license. Recording rights, co-commissioning arrangements, and other terms are negotiated individually. I retain the copyright to all commissioned works.
Can I commission an arrangement of an existing work?+
Yes — I regularly create new arrangements, transcriptions, and versions of existing works for different ensembles. This can be a cost-effective way to bring a piece you admire to your specific instrumentation. The process is similar but typically faster, since the musical material already exists.
