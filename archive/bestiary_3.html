About the Bestiary
The story behind the Bestiary
Origin story
+

The Compositional Bestiary began as a private notebook — a collection of techniques I'd encountered in the repertoire, in lessons, in late-night score study, and in my own fumbling experiments at the piano. Every time I discovered a compositional move that excited me, I'd write it down on an index card with a brief description and a provocation: what would happen if I used this technique as the seed for an entire piece?

Over the course of my PhD research, that stack of index cards grew from a personal reference into something more structured. I began categorizing them by musical dimension — harmony, rhythm, timbre, form — and noticing that certain cards worked beautifully in combination, while others created productive friction. The idea of a game emerged naturally: what if you drew cards at random and let the constraints define the piece?

I started using the cards in my own composition practice, then with students, then in workshops at festivals and universities. The feedback was consistent: composers at every level — from undergraduates to established professionals — found that the cards broke through creative blocks, surfaced techniques they'd never considered, and turned the often solitary act of composition into something more playful and social.

The deck went through four major revisions. Early versions were too prescriptive — they told you exactly what to do, which killed the creative spark. Later versions were too vague, more fortune cookie than compositional tool. The current edition sits in what I think of as the productive middle: each card names a real technique drawn from the literature, describes it clearly enough that you could use it immediately, and then poses a question or prompt that invites you to make it your own.

The word "bestiary" felt right because medieval bestiaries weren't just catalogs of animals — they were repositories of wonder, moral instruction, and imagination, all wrapped in the language of natural observation. The Compositional Bestiary aspires to the same spirit: a catalog of musical creatures, each one real and documented, but each one also an invitation to imagine what else might be possible.

Who is this for?+
Composers at any level — from students encountering these techniques for the first time to professionals looking to break out of habitual patterns. It's also been used successfully by songwriters, sound designers, improvisers, and music educators. If you make music and sometimes wish someone would just tell you what to write next, this is for you.
How is this different from Oblique Strategies?+
Brian Eno and Peter Schmidt's Oblique Strategies are deliberately abstract and open to interpretation — beautiful for disrupting creative habits, but not grounded in specific compositional technique. The Bestiary cards are concrete and actionable: each one names a real technique from the compositional literature, explains how it works, and suggests a specific way to deploy it. The Bestiary also has structured play modes (spreads, workshop swaps, collaborative rounds) that Oblique Strategies doesn't offer.
Can I use this for teaching?+
Absolutely — many of the play modes were developed in educational settings. The deck works especially well for composition seminars, group workshops, and one-on-one lessons. Some teachers use the cards to structure assignments (draw 3 cards, compose a 2-minute study using all three techniques). Institutional licensing for multiple digital copies is available — get in touch.

Yes. The digital edition includes all future card additions at no extra cost. New cards are added periodically as I encounter or develop new techniques. Physical edition owners get access to print-at-home PDFs of new cards so they can expand their deck.
